Executive Summary

From Spreadsheet Ritual to Deterministic Decision Infrastructure

Every quarter, growth teams burn their most expensive hours on the same ritual: exporting platform data, wrestling it into spreadsheets, arguing about which rows to trust, and negotiating budget splits from gut feel dressed up as analysis. The output quality depends entirely on who did the work, how tired they were, and which anomalies they happened to notice.

This project replaces that ritual with deterministic decision infrastructure. The UA Command Center is a browser-based engine that ingests a raw quarterly campaign export and — with zero human intervention — audits the data, quantifies signal trustworthiness, surfaces the judgments a senior UA manager would make, and produces a fully-reasoned $50,000/month budget allocation with pre-committed course-correction tripwires.

The design contract: same schema in → same elite quality of UA judgment out. Every quarter. No spreadsheets. No human error. No dependence on who's at the keyboard.

Every threshold that constitutes "judgment" — what makes a signal trustworthy, when a campaign earns scaling, where demand capture must stop, when a budget sliver becomes waste — is codified in a single, documented configuration block. The institution's UA logic now lives in reviewable code, not in any individual's head. When Q2's export lands, the machine re-derives every verdict in under a second, and the team's time shifts from producing the analysis to challenging it.

Proof of the standard, on real Q1 data: the system independently isolated a $10,149 tracking outage as a financial write-off, quarantined 78 rows of corrupted cohort data before they could poison LTV math, identified our largest-volume campaign as a vanity-CPI trap and cut its entire $38K/month to $0 — while pinning our highest-ROAS campaigns at their structural ceilings rather than naively scaling them, landing on a plan with a 137.4% projected D30 ROAS.

Part 1 · Growth Intelligence

The UA Insight & Command Dashboard

1.1 Architecture from a Business Standpoint

The entire system is one HTML file, zero backend. This is not an engineering shortcut — it is a deliberate operating decision with three commercial consequences:

PropertyBusiness Impact
Total data privacySpend, revenue, and cohort economics are processed entirely in the local browser. Financial data never touches a server, a vendor, or a network. There is no data processing agreement to negotiate because there is no data transfer.
Zero onboarding frictionAny stakeholder — CMO, finance partner, new UA hire — opens the file, drops the CSV, and has the full quarterly read in seconds. No accounts, no BI licenses, no IT tickets, no training.
Instant, repeatable processing5,000+ rows are parsed, audited, aggregated, and converted into an allocation plan in under a second, identically on every upload. The marginal cost of re-running the entire quarterly analysis is zero.

1.2 The Programmatic Data Audit Layer

Principle: never trust the export. Platform data is a hostile witness — it double-counts, drops attribution, and restates history. The dashboard runs twelve cleaning rules on every upload, and every rule reports publicly in an Audit Log — including the rules that caught nothing. The team sees exactly what was excluded, adjusted, or projected, and why. Nothing is silently "fixed." Two interventions from Q1 demonstrate the standard of care:

The $10,149 TikTok tracking outage — a write-off, not a performance signal

Forty-two rows (TikTok_VVO_Cold, Feb 10–16 — a contiguous cluster, the fingerprint of a postback outage rather than random loss) showed real spend with exactly zero recorded installs. The naive treatments are both wrong: leave the rows in and the campaign's CPI and ROAS are punished for a measurement failure; delete them and $10,149 of real money vanishes from the books. The system does neither. The spend is preserved as a "Wasted/Outage Spend" line in the financial ledger — visible on the topline — while the rows are excluded from every rate metric. Measurement failures are accounted for as costs; they are never allowed to masquerade as performance.

78 rows of impossible retention curves — quarantined before they touch LTV math

Retention is a survival curve; it can only decay (D1 ≥ D7 ≥ D30). The audit layer flagged 78 rows — concentrated in TikTok — where D30 retention exceeded D7: physically impossible, and proof the cohort tracking is corrupt. These rows keep their spend and install counts (the ad-platform side is intact) but are surgically excluded from retention averages, from the D30/D7 revenue-projection multiplier, and from projected ROAS. This is the difference between a dashboard and decision infrastructure: we refuse to build revenue models on cohorts we know are broken.

The same layer deduplicated 26 replayed export rows, ledgered 12 negative-spend refund credits (~$1,579), floored 5 impossible revenue regressions, and ignores the platform's pre-computed CPI column entirely — every rate metric is recomputed from raw counts.

Cohort maturity, handled honestly. 1,661 rows (all of March) were missing D30 revenue for the only legitimate reason: those cohorts were too young to have any. Dropping them would erase the newest month; trusting blanks would zero out real revenue. Instead, matured cohorts revealed a remarkably stable D30/D7 revenue multiplier (2.22×), which projects the immature tail — labeled as a projection everywhere it appears, with a scheduled Day-60 validation against reality.

1.3 Performance Views & Statistical Guardrails

Performance is presented at three altitudes — channel, campaign, adset — color-coded against explicit targets (D30 ROAS breakeven at 100%, CPI ≤ $2.50, CTR ≥ 2%, D7 retention ≥ 12%). More importantly, every entity carries a statistical confidence tier derived from its volume (margin of error ≈ 1.96/√installs):

TierQualificationDecision Rights
HIGH≥ $2K spend, ±10% margin of errorDecision-grade: can be scaled or killed on its own numbers.
MEDIUM≥ $500 spend, ±20% margin of errorDirectional only.
LOWAnything weakerNever scaled, never permanently killed on its own data — it simply loses budget priority.

This guardrail exists because the most expensive mistakes in UA are made chasing high-variance, low-sample numbers — the adset that ran 40% ROAS on $180 of spend. The dashboard makes that trap structurally impossible: the allocator refuses to act on LOW-confidence signals, and every quoted average carries its sample size. The system also flags what it cannot normalize: our four channels report on three different attribution windows (7d-click/1d-view, 30d-click, 1d-tap), so cross-channel ROAS gaps under ~20% are labeled measurement noise, not performance.

1.4 Deterministic Budget Optimization: The $50K/Month Two-Stage Allocator

The allocation engine encodes a truth most optimizers miss: not all ROAS is created equal.

Stage 1 — Demand Capture is funded first, and capped hard. Brand and retargeting campaigns post spectacular ROAS — Google_Search_Brand at 479% projected D30, Meta_Retargeting at 286% — precisely because they harvest demand created elsewhere: organic brand queries and the retargeting pool that prospecting fills. That inventory is structurally bounded. A naive ROAS-maximizer would pour the budget here and watch marginal returns collapse into broad-match queries and stale audience segments. Our allocator funds each to full historical coverage and not one dollar more: Meta_Retargeting $7,618/mo, Google_Search_Brand $4,897/mo, ASA_Brand $2,932/mo. Ceilings rise only on evidence the pool itself grew.

Stage 2 — Every remaining dollar flows to Prospecting by projected D30 ROAS. The residual $34,553/mo water-fills the demand-creating campaigns proportional to projected D30 ROAS, within per-verdict caps (scaling capped at 2× historical to avoid the auction-efficiency cliff; reduced campaigns at 0.5×). Prospecting is deliberately privileged as a class: it is the top of the funnel that keeps both the brand-search volume and the retargeting pool alive.

The pruning loop — no budget confetti. Any surviving allocation below $2,000/month is converted to CUT and redistributed. A $900/month line item can't buy statistically usable signal; it is fragmentation wearing a diversification costume. The loop reruns until every funded campaign has a viable test budget.

The kill that funds the plan

TikTok_VVO_Cold — our single largest line at $38K/month — was cut to $0. The vanity-CPI trap in full: the portfolio's cheapest installs ($0.43 CPI, 266K installs) with 4.4% D7 retention and 18.5% projected D30 ROAS. Cheap tourists, not users. Critically, the outage cleanup improved this campaign's measured metrics before the verdict — the system killed it on fundamentals, fairly measured, which makes the decision defensible in any room.

1.5 The 30/60/90-Day Operational Tripwires

The plan admits, in advance, exactly where it could be wrong — and pre-commits the response:

Day 30Per-campaign guardrails computed from current metrics: revisit any line whose CPI drifts >1.25× or whose D7 ROAS falls below 0.8× of its Q1 baseline on ≥50% of month-1 spend. For demand capture, rising CPI at flat budget = pool saturation → trim, don't chase. Plan-level: blended D7 ROAS below ~49.8% (vs 62.3% expected) forces an immediate re-run, not a quarter-end postmortem. Any spend day with zero recorded installs escalates to the MMP within 48 hours.
Day 60Month-1 cohorts are now D30-mature: validate the 2.22× projection multiplier against reality. Outside ±20%, every projected verdict inherits the error — reclassify with the corrected curve. Demand-capture caps rise only on impression-share-lost-to-budget evidence, in ≤15% steps.
Day 90Full replan: drop the new quarter's CSV in; every verdict re-derives. CUT campaigns may re-enter only through a $2,000–$5,000/mo test cell with a fixed creative hypothesis — never at legacy budgets.

Part 3 · AI Working Process

Reverse-Engineering the AI Co-Pilot Workflow

3.1 The Dynamic: Chief Architect + Agile Execution Engine

This system was built in a single working session using Claude Code — but the operating model matters more than the speed. The human operated as Chief Architect: setting the decision framework, supplying the domain physics of UA, and auditing every judgment the machine encoded. The AI operated as an Agile Execution Engine: profiling raw data before writing a line of logic, implementing the pipeline, self-verifying in a live browser, and returning proof rather than promises.

The velocity gain was real — institutional-grade decision infrastructure in hours, not sprints. But the quality gain came from the loop: every human correction was immediately codified into permanent, testable rules. Senior judgment stopped being advice and became infrastructure.

3.2 Critical Human Guardrails: Where Domain Knowledge Overrode the Machine

1 · Tracking outages are write-offs, not deletions

The baseline engineering instinct for "spend with zero installs" is to drop the rows as dirty data. The senior override: that is $10,149 of real money — deleting it falsifies the books, while keeping it defames the campaign. The taught rule — preserve as a financial ledger line, exclude from rate metrics, and read date-contiguity as outage evidence — is a UA controller's instinct that no general-purpose model defaults to.

2 · Monotonicity as a hard data law

The human mandate: retention curves that rise over time are corrupt, and corrupted cohorts must never feed LTV projections — even when the row's spend data is fine. Notably, the collaboration sharpened the rule in both directions: the human specified "D7 > D1" from memory; the AI verified against raw data, found zero such rows, and located the actual violation (D30 > D7, 85 raw rows) — then implemented the general law covering every inversion. Human principle, machine precision. Neither alone gets it right.

3 · Demand capture does not scale — no matter what the ROAS says

The AI's first allocator did what every optimizer does: it saw 479% ROAS on brand search and scaled it 2×. The senior correction is the kind of scar tissue that only comes from having watched brand campaigns saturate: that ROAS is harvested, not created; the inventory is bounded by organic query volume and pool size; cap it at historical volume and push every marginal dollar into demand creation. This single override restructured the entire allocation engine into the two-stage system — and cut the plan's projected ROAS by a third, on purpose, in exchange for a forecast that will survive contact with reality.

3.3 Division of Labor: What Stayed Human, What Was Delegated

Kept Strictly in Human HandsDelegated to the AI
Risk thresholds: what ROAS earns scaling, what confidence earns trust, where the kill-line sitsData pipeline: parsing, cleaning rules, joins, aggregation math
Growth-model heuristics: demand capture vs. prospecting, pool-saturation logic, payback-curve assumptionsStatistical mechanics: confidence intervals, weighted aggregates, projection multipliers
The meaning of anomalies: outage vs. collapse, refund vs. loss, immature vs. missingVerification: live-browser regression testing of every output against the raw file
Final accountability for the $50K/monthUI and visualization: tables, charts, audit-log presentation

The principle behind the split: the machine executes judgment; it does not originate it. Strategic thresholds encode risk appetite and market scar tissue — they are accountability decisions, and accountability cannot be delegated. What can be delegated is everything that must be done perfectly and identically ten thousand times: the parsing, the math, the checks, the rendering. The result is a system where the growth leader's judgment is applied with machine consistency — and where every quarter, the machine hands back the one thing spreadsheets never could: time to think.
